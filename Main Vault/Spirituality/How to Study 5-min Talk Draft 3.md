##### Introduction (1m 20s)
I thought I could start by describing a situation I've found myself in, and you could see if you relate. 

I'd had a long day, and I was tired, but it was Friday evening and I hadn't studied for the Watchtower yet. So, I sat down at my desk, and started going through the article. I was highlighting, answering questions, and finding a bunch of points that I remember thinking, "oh that's really interesting." Once I finished it, I went back to whatever random stuff I was doing before, and then immediately forgot everything that I'd just read. Not a single point remained. I couldn't tell you the name of the article, I'm not joking.

Granted, maybe that's just the product of my fried attention span. But it's true that sometimes we might feel like we're just going through the motions of studying without really getting much out of it after the fact. The broader point of studying in general is to get those points out of it, so not getting much that really applies to you personally can at times feel disappointing, or unproductive. 

So, how do we avoid that? Well, thankfully, the organization has provided some helpful pieces of advice to help us to get more out of our personal study. So, in this talk, we're going to be briefly considering 4 of those reminders. They're to:
1. Preview the article
2. Engage with the material
3. Review the main points
4. Apply what you learn

##### Body 1 (40s)
First, preview the article. If you're studying for the Watchtower, instead of simply working from paragraph 1 through to the end, briefly take a moment to look over the article ahead of time. That could mean considering the wording of the title, the subheadings, or the illustrations. 

And, if you listen to the audio descriptions of the pictures, not only will you get a lot of small easy-to-miss details, but I'm pretty sure Dion will give you 5 gold stars, and I wouldn't want to miss out on that. Previewing helps to paint a mental picture of the overall theme of the article, and gives your study a clear direction out the gate. So, when you're studying, you know what points to look for.

##### Body 2 (45s)
Second, engage with the material. So, if the article includes any printed questions, spend some time finding and marking the answers. It can also be helpful to mark any other points you appreciated; these could be points you could apply to yourself, points you could use in the ministry, points you could share with someone you know, or something else. 

But what do you do if you encounter something you don't understand right away? Well, instead of letting it sidetrack you, make a note of it. In many cases, whatever it is will be clarified later in the material. If it isn't, you could come back to it another time and do some more research on it.

It's helpful to engage with the material like this because it keeps your study active, which makes it more likely these points will actually stick with you. 

##### Body 3 (40s)
Third, review the main points. The apostle Paul was a great example of this. When writing to the Hebrews, he would pause, and clarify the primary facts he wanted the audience to take away. In fact, at Hebrews 8:1, he's quoted as saying literally, "this is the main point of what we are saying." By emphasizing the main points like this, he prepared his readers to meditate on them. 

In a similar way, when we study, we can occasionally pause and focus on the primary messages we're meant to take away. If we do that, we'll have an easier time remembering those main points and applying them.

##### Body 4 (25s)
Which brings us right into our final point: apply what you've learned. When we learn something, we can  ask ourselves questions like, "How should this affect my attitude and my goals? How can I apply this in solving a problem, making a decision, or reaching a goal? How could I use this in my family life, in the field ministry, or in the congregation?" Thinking of opportunities to apply the points you learn will help you to remember and act on them.

##### Conclusion (30s)
So, what were our 4 main points? First, preview the article. That could mean looking at the title, subheadings, and illustrations ahead of time. Second, engage with the material. Which means answering questions, and marking points you appreciate. Third was to review the main points. Which means pausing to discern the primary messages we're meant to take away. And the last point was to apply what you've learned. Which means to meditate in advance on ways to apply the points you learn. 

Using these points will help you to have more productive personal study sessions that take full advantage of all the spiritual food that we receive.
