##### Introduction (1m)
I thought I could start by describing a situation I've found myself in, and you could see if you relate. It's been a long day, maybe you're tired, but you finally find some time for some personal study. You sit down, and start reading through the material. As you're reading, you're highlighting points, answering questions, and taking notes, and thinking, "man I'm doing great." You finish up, and get back to your day. Then, not 10 minutes later, you have absolutely no idea what you just read. 

Maybe that's the product of my fried attention span, but it's true that sometimes we might feel like we're just going through the motions of studying without getting much out of it. That can be disappointing, and can feel unproductive. 

So, how do you have more productive personal studies? How do you avoid just going through the motions? That's what we're going to cover in this talk. We're going to look at four simple habits that can make it easier to focus and take to heart what you're learning. Those are to:
1. Preview
2. Engage
3. Review
4. Apply

##### Body
First, previewing. If you're studying for the Watchtower, instead of simply working from paragraph 1 through to the end, briefly take a moment to consider the wording of the title, the subheadings, and any illustrations. Then, you could ask yourself what you expect to learn. This will sort of paint a mental picture of the overall theme of the article, which will give your study more direction.

Second, engage with the material. So, if the material includes any printed questions, spend some time finding and marking the answers. Additionally, it can be helpful to mark any other points you appreciated; these could be points you could apply to yourself, points you could use in the ministry, points you could share with someone you know, or something else. 

However, sometimes, while you're reading, you might encounter something you don't get right away. Instead of letting them sidetrack you, make a note to give the point further consideration later. If the point isn't clarified later in the material, you could do more research later. If you participate in the material like this, you'll get a much fuller understanding of the article.

Third, review the main points. When writing to the Hebrews, the apostle Paul would pause, and clarify the primary facts he wanted the audience to take away. Doing this helped those important points to stick in the minds of the readers. Similarly, when we study, if we occasionally pause and discern the main points, we'll better understand the development of the theme, have an easier time remembering them, and have more motivation for acting on what we've learned. 

Finally, apply what you've learned. When you learn something, ask yourself questions like, "How should this affect my attitude and my goals? How can I apply this in solving a problem, making a decision, or reaching a goal? How could I use this with my family, in the field ministry, or in the congregation?" Putting thought into situations where you could actually use what you're learning will help you to remember and apply it. 

##### Conclusion
So, what were the 4 points? Preview the title, subheadings, and illustrations, engage with any questions and what you want to remember, review the main points, and apply it in your life. Doing all of these will help you to avoid just going through the motions; instead, you'll have more productive study sessions that stick with you, instead of forgetting about them after 10 minutes.
