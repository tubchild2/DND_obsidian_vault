### Progression
##### Difficulty Curves
- A difficulty curve is a rough chart that shows player ability vs challenge over time
- Difficulty is incredibly important in game design. Overcoming challenges and feeling your skill grow is what has made games like Dark Souls, Celeste, and Silksong so popular. 
- Games in which player ability is too far above challenge are usually less interesting, because you don't feel yourself improving. 
- Games in which player ability is too far below challenge are usually too frustrating, because (once again) you don't feel yourself improving. 
- There are exceptions to these rules; some games that want to focus on story or power fantasies may prefer to make things really easy. Some games that focus on providing insurmountable challenge for challenge's sake, like Getting Over It or Dark Souls 3, absolutely work despite being traditionally unbalanced. 
- The aim is for difficulty to rise and fall around the player's skill. They get challenged, they overcome the challenge, they feel powerful, rinse and repeat. That's how it works in pretty much every game ever made. 

##### Minecraft's Curve
- Minecraft's difficulty was originally designed in such a way that things take a lot of time. By the time you reach the end, you'd have access to enchanted diamond armor. 
- However, now, you can get that and way more immediately with villagers and skip the first third of the game to go straight to the nether. Then, with redstone farms, Netherite, shields, totems, and eventually Elytras you can get even stronger. So, if in modern versions you're starting the game at a point in ability that you wouldn't have reached until the very end in 1.8 and just getting stronger, Minecraft's challenge needs to rise to compensate. However, it doesn't. The game was designed around one system of progression, but radically changed to make players too powerful. 

##### Consequences
- So, with little challenge, there's no feeling of progress being made. That makes the game feel very one-note and uninteresting. While this much power is great for intrinsically motivated players who enjoy building and creativity, like Mason, it's not so great for everyone else that wants goals, excitement, or challenge. 
- Additionally, due to the new progression system, there's a right way to do everything. In 1.8, you could spend the first hour mining, farming, building, or exploring, and they were all similarly balanced because you'd need to do all 3 to beat the game. Given time, all three of those players would progress at the same rate. In modern versions, whichever player finds a village progresses leagues faster. 
- Here's the issue; having an objectively best way to play Minecraft is counter to it's original thesis. I quote the original Minecraft trailer from 2012, "Let's go wherever you want to go. Climb the tallest mountains. Venture down to the darkest caves. Build anything you want. Day or night. Rain or shine. Because this is the most significant sandbox you'll ever set foot in... No one can tell you what you can or cannot do." 
- Comparing classic Minecraft with modern Minecraft feels like comparing two different games. It's not just because of nostalgia, it's because of the changes they've made to progression that not only move the player's abilities beyond the challenge the game was designed for, but because the systems put in place disincentivize creativity by offering an objective best way to play the game. 
- If you think I'm being dramatic, that's fair. But think about this. How many servers have you played on in the past 7 years that have devolved into the follow rough loop: Spawn, find a village, trade for gear, go to the nether, get Netherite, build redstone farms, beat the ender dragon, get maxed out, lose interest. 



Main Points
- Supply and Demand
	- Item economies are complicated
	- Demand is how much you want / need something, and supply is how much you have
	- The ratio between these two creates the illusion of rarity in a virtual economy
	- To maintain the same sense of rarity while updating a game, that ratio of supply/demand needs to be in-tact
	- Iron's demand has been drastically increased due to being used in so many more crafting recipes. However, it's supply has been decreased due to the Caves and Cliffs update moving primary iron veins into mountains. While great in theory, that means that in order to get the same amount of resources you would strip mining in 1.8, you need to do twice as many trips. Iron's perceived rarity / value has, therefore, risen drastically. 
	- Diamond's demand was radically worsened. It was originally used for enchanting, armor, and gear. Since you can get all of those things faster from villagers, it no longer has much real demand. With the new ore distribution, diamonds are in greater supply. So, their perceived value has decreased.
	- What's the big deal? The big deal is that it changes how players feel when acquiring these resources. It makes things less exciting and more grind-focused. 
- Aesthetic and Tonality Damage
	- Classic Minecraft had a uniquely unsettling and lonely atmosphere
	- Modern Minecraft is bright, bubbly, and colorful. It's appealing to kids too heavily in its marketing. 
	- The world's are oversaturated with structures, making each discovery less exciting and mysterious. Additionally, the loot is generic and underwhelming.
